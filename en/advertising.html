Advertising in 2085:
A Retro-Futuristic Immersion

In 2085, advertising merges technology and nostalgia. Interactive holograms captivate passersby, while retro design evokes objects from the past. This sensory experience transforms advertising into a spectacle where past and future meet, creating an unprecedented emotional connection.

Article written on October 17, 2024, by Camille Norrito
Reading time: 5-6 min

City in 2085

In 2085, advertising has reached new heights, blending cutting-edge technology with a nostalgic nod to past eras. Major cities like New York and Tokyo have become true theaters of illusion, where the line between reality and virtuality blurs. Times Square is no longer just a mosaic of bright billboards; it’s an intersection where holographic objects appear to break free from screens, mingling with passersby to catch their attention. These experiences, at the crossroads of science fiction and retro dreams, are reinventing the way brands interact with their audience.

Advertising in Minority Report

Inspired by works like Minority Report, where holographic ads directly address individuals, future advertising pushes the boundaries of immersion. By 2085, holograms no longer simply project images; they interact, track pedestrians, analyze their behavior, and adapt to their emotions. A jogger might see a holographic pair of running shoes alongside them, encouraging them to explore the brand’s latest collection. These dynamic ads seamlessly integrate into the urban environment, playing on surprise and wonder, much like giant holographic creatures seemingly emerging from skyscrapers in Tokyo’s Shibuya district.


However, advertising in 2085 isn’t just about innovation. The phenomenon of retro design, already prevalent in fashion and architecture, has found its place in this world as well. Futuristic display technologies are wrapped in a vintage aesthetic: screens resembling old 1970s TVs display 8K-quality holograms, while ads evoke 1990s flip phones, now coveted items in this hyperconnected era. Brands use this nostalgia to foster an emotional connection, playing on the contrast between the old and the new.
